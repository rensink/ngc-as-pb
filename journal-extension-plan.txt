- definition of well-formed condition and well-formed variant.

Let   $c=(R,(a_1,c_1)\ccdots (a_w,c_w)$ be an ab-condition. Let $\nodes{c}$ be inductively defined as $\nodes{c} = V_R \bigcup_{i=1}^w \nodes{c_i}$. then we say that $c$ \emph{is well formed} iff for all $i\in \{1, \ldots, w\}$ $c_i$ is well formed and the sets $V_c, \nodes{c_1}, \ldots, \nodes{c_w}$ are pairwise disjoint.

Given conditions $c=(R,(a_1,c_1)\ccdots (a_w,c_w)$ and $c'=(R',(a'_1,c'_1)\ccdots (a'_{w'},c'_{w'})$, a \emph{s-iso} (structural iso) $m: c \leftrighrarrow c'$ is an iso (in \cat{Graph}) $m: R \leftrightarrow R'$ such that $w = w'$ and for all $i \in \{1, \ldots, w\} there are s-isos $m_i: c_i \leftrightarrow c'_i$ such that $m; a'_i = a_i; m_i$.

Given a condition $c in \AC{R}$, a \emph{well-formed variant} of $c$ is a condition $c' \AC{R}$ such that $id_R$ is a structural iso form $c$ to $c'$.

=> As expected, a condition $c$ and any of its well-formed variants are not distinguishable using satisfaction / have exactly the same model. This will be an immediate consequence of the properties of morphisms introduced later.
